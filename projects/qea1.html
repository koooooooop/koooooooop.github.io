This project documents the design, mathematical modeling and physical construction of a model ship with specific performance requirements. Our goal was to create a vessel capable of stable floating, carrying a 10kg load, and maintaining stability with a capsizing angle between 120-140 degrees.

Through rigorous mathematical calculations in MATLAB and precise 3D modeling in SolidWorks, we were able to translate theoretical concepts into a functional physical prototype. The ship, nicknamed "Cheese Cream" due to its distinctive shape, successfully met all design criteria during testing.

Abstract

This report discusses the process of designing, simulating, and manufacturing a model ship that can meet specific performance requirements, including stable floating, carrying a 10kg load, and having a capsizing angle of 120-140 degrees. The technical background section explains basic concepts of ship electric propulsion, center of buoyancy, center of gravity, and righting moments.

To define the surface equation for the hull, we iterated through many equations and diagrams, detailing the hull design layout. The mathematical calculations for the stability angle of the final design include calculating the ship's center of gravity, center of buoyancy, and the righting moment between these two points.

After creating a model for the final hull design, we recreated the ship in engineering, then laser-cut the keel and assembled the hull. During testing, the ship successfully floated flat and was able to correct its capsizing angle.

Technical Background

Electric Ship Propulsion

The main components of a ship's electric propulsion system are the propeller, motor, power supply (generator, battery), prime mover, and its control and regulation devices. Electric propulsion can be classified differently according to its use, the type of prime mover adopted, and its control method. However, from an electrical concept, it can be divided into three categories: DC electric propulsion, AC electric propulsion, and AC/DC electric propulsion.

Ship electric propulsion has a history of nearly a hundred years from development to application. Ships equipped with electric propulsion devices have obvious advantages over those with traditional mechanical propulsion methods in terms of maneuverability, reliability, and economy, which has led to their increasingly widespread use in modern shipbuilding.

Manufacturing Process

Design Approach

First, we determined the type of ship and the functional form we wanted to achieve—mimicking the features of a destroyer. We established multi-level modeling to make the ship's parameter conditions more intuitive. This included center of gravity and buoyancy (righting moment), force analysis model (ship speed dynamics), and three-dimensional solid model.

Detailed Modeling Process

Selection of Modeling Software

The project used Rhino software for 3D modeling, which can precisely simulate every detail of the hull, including curves, angles, and seams. The actual production referenced related product models of ship types.

Hull Structure Material Planning

Foam board was selected as the material for the ship's external shape due to its higher density, greater hardness, and suitability for manufacturing architectural models. Its advantages included ease of manual cutting while ensuring hardness with some elasticity, allowing for better shaping of the ship's form.

Keel Design Process

Design Concept and Initial Sketches:
The keel design began with a deep understanding of the ship's overall structure and functional requirements. We first established the basic dimensions and shape of the hull, then drew preliminary sketches of the keel design based on these parameters.

Detail Adjustment and Optimization:
Based on the 3D model, we made multiple adjustments to the structural details of the keel, particularly optimizing the spacing, thickness, and connection methods of the ribs to improve the overall strength and stability of the structure.

Technical Challenges and Iterative Improvements:
The initial keel design encountered issues of overly dense rib spacing, insufficient contact area between ribs and foam board, and inaccurate dimensions. To solve these problems, we went through design iterations, testing the feasibility of each design through actual assembly simulations.

Motor Selection and Configuration

Motor specifications

Figure 13: Selected motor specifications and configuration details

Motor Type and Parameter Selection:
We selected high-efficiency DC motors for good speed regulation and torque output characteristics. When selecting motors, we considered rated power, speed, and torque to ensure they met the power requirements of the hull.

Motor Installation and Integration:
After multiple experiments and adjustments, the motors were installed at the rear center axis position of the hull to maintain balance and improve drive efficiency.

Reflection and Conclusion

Experiences and Gains

Technology Application and Innovation:
This project allowed us to become proficient with MATLAB and SolidWorks, integrating these tools to solve complex engineering problems. MATLAB's computational capabilities were essential for calculating stability parameters and simulating dynamic behavior, while SolidWorks facilitated precise visualization and structural testing.

The exploration of laser cutting technology and various new materials (foam board, polyurethane foam) enhanced our manufacturing process, resulting in more precise components and improved physical properties like plasticity, waterproofing, and controllability.

Challenges and Solutions

Design and Manufacturing Challenges:
The initial keel design presented significant assembly difficulties due to inaccurate parameters (rib spacing, dimensions). This experience highlighted the importance of thorough simulation and parameter testing before physical implementation.

Waterproofing presented another challenge, as our initial approach using tape and foam fillers proved insufficient for long-term use. This led us to explore more robust waterproofing methods for future iterations.

Results and Achievements

The stability testing exceeded our expectations, with the ship maintaining balance at capsizing angles of 120-140 degrees. This validated both our mathematical model and physical design implementation, confirming the success of our engineering approach.

Each iterative improvement was based on rigorous testing and evaluation, demonstrating the value of evidence-based methodology in engineering practice.

Key Project Achievements

This project successfully combined theoretical modeling with practical implementation, resulting in a functional ship model that met all design requirements. Through iterative design and testing, we overcame numerous challenges to create a stable, load-bearing vessel with precisely calculated performance characteristics.
